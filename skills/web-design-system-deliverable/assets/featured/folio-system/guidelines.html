Guidelines

Usage & Governance

Rules for applying the Folio system consistently. Brand strategy, voice and tone, typography, color, spacing, image presentation, motion, composition, and accessibility — each section traces decisions back to the founding principle: the photograph speaks first.

01 — Brand Strategy

Positioning & Personality

Folio exists for photographers and galleries who present work at the highest level. The system creates an environment where images command undivided attention — where the interface disappears into the wall.

Positioning
Premium / Aspirational

For photographers and galleries who present work at the highest level, Folio is the design system that frames images with the same care as a museum wall — unlike template-based portfolio builders which prioritize features over presentation. We earn this through restraint, precision, and deference to the image.

Audience
Represented Photographers

Photographers represented by agencies (Magnum, VII, Panos), exhibiting at galleries (Gagosian, Foam, White Cube), and publishing through editorial channels (Aperture, British Journal of Photography). The audience expects the quality of a printed monograph translated to screen.

Competitors
Template Builders

Squarespace, Format, PhotoShelter. These serve photographers adequately but their templates are recognizable, their typographic systems are limited, and they treat the image as one element among many rather than as the protagonist.

Differentiator
Gallery-Grade Restraint

Where competitors add features, Folio removes them. The differentiator is subtraction: fewer colors, lighter weights, sharper geometry, more silence. The system is opinionated about what it will not do.

02 — Voice & Tone

How Folio Speaks

Voice is constant. Tone shifts by context. In all cases, the language is subordinate to the image — it frames, it does not narrate.

Measured

Every word earns its place. Sentences are short. Adjectives are rare. Let the work speak.

Not: Curt, cold, or withholding information the viewer needs.

Authoritative

Statements, not suggestions. "View Exhibition" not "You might enjoy this show." Confidence without arrogance.

Not: Dismissive, elitist, or gatekeeping.

Understated

No exclamation marks. No superlatives. Restraint in language mirrors restraint in design.

Not: Monotone or so understated that it feels detached.

Precise

"Silver gelatin print, 40 x 50 cm, edition of 15" — not "beautiful large print." Specificity replaces decoration.

Not: Jargon-heavy or inaccessible to non-specialists.

03 — Typography

04 — Color Usage

The Near-Achromatic Principle

Photography provides the color. The interface stays neutral. The warm off-white canvas and near-black text create a gallery wall — a surface that enhances the image rather than competing with it.

Accent: Terracotta

Used only for: text links, focus rings, active navigation states, primary CTA fill. Never as decoration, background tint (except alerts), or border color on structural elements. The accent appears sparingly — if more than 5% of a page's surface area is accent-colored, reduce it.

Canvas: Warm Off-White

The primary surface. Not pure #ffffff — the warm undertone (#f4f2ed) references archival paper and gallery walls. Pure white appears only on elevated surfaces (cards, modals) to create subtle depth without shadows.

Text: Near-Black

Primary text at #141311. Not pure black — the warm undertone matches the canvas. Secondary text at #6b665e for body content, tertiary at #8a857b for metadata and labels. The text hierarchy uses value (lightness) exclusively, never color.

Borders: Opacity-Based

Borders use rgba() at 4-14% opacity, never solid hex values. This allows them to adapt to any surface. Premium photography sites (Gagosian, David Zwirner) use whitespace instead of borders wherever possible — a border is a last resort.

Dark Mode Rationale

Dark mode uses near-black (#0a0a09) — not pure #000000. The warm off-white text (#e8e5df) prevents the harshness of white-on-black. Photographs often display better against dark backgrounds (the spotlight effect), making dark mode the preferred viewing context for portfolio pages. However, Folio does not auto-enable dark mode — the viewer controls the experience.

05 — Spacing & Layout

Generous Silence

White space is the wall that gives the print room to breathe. Section spacing starts at 96px and scales to 160px for exhibition-level presentation. This is the single biggest differentiator between a gallery website and a template.

Tier Context Section Spacing Card Padding Body Size
Exhibition Portfolio pages, hero sections, landing pages 160px 32px 19px
Standard About pages, exhibition lists, publication credits 96px 24px 17px
Archive Print inventory, edition tables, search results 64px 16px 14px
Photography Layout Patterns
Full-Bleed

Image spans the full viewport width. No margins, no padding. Used for hero images, featured work, and immersive viewing. The image IS the section.

Contained

Image sits within the content column with generous margins. Used for editorial context, inline images within text, and when the image needs the framing of surrounding whitespace.

Asymmetric Grid

Variable-size images in a masonry or asymmetric grid. One image larger than the others. Used for project overviews and selected work sections. Never uniform thumbnails.

06 — Image Presentation

Framing the Work

Every decision about image presentation serves one goal: the photograph commands the viewer's full attention. The interface around it is invisible.

Aspect Ratios

Preserve the photographer's original aspect ratio whenever possible. Do not force-crop to fit a grid. If a uniform grid is required, use 3:2 for landscape and 2:3 for portrait — these match standard photographic paper sizes.

Hero: original aspect or 16:9 / 21:9
Card: 3:2 (landscape) or 2:3 (portrait)
Thumbnail: 1:1 (square crop, face-detect focal point)
Open Graph: 1.91:1

Captions

Captions appear below the image in Crimson Pro italic, 14px, secondary color. For gallery/lightbox views, captions overlay the bottom edge with a subtle gradient scrim. Captions are never bold, never uppercase, never larger than 14px.

Font: Crimson Pro italic, 14px
Color: --text-secondary
Format: Title, Year / Medium / Dimensions / Edition
Hover Behavior

Interactive images dim on hover — filter: brightness(0.82). This is the Magnum Photos convention. No scale, no shadow, no overlay text on hover for gallery images. The dimming signals interactivity while preserving the image's integrity.

Hover: filter: brightness(0.82)
Duration: var(--duration-normal) / 280ms
Easing: var(--ease-out)
Never: scale, shadow, color overlay
Lightbox

Full-screen viewing uses a near-black background (#0a0a09). The image is centered with generous padding (minimum 48px from viewport edges). Navigation arrows are minimal — thin strokes, 50% opacity, visible on hover only. Close button top-right.

Background: var(--color-black)
Image padding: 48px minimum
Controls: 50% opacity, visible on hover
Transition: fade 280ms var(--ease-out)
07 — Motion

Restrained Movement

Photography is still. The interface respects that stillness. Motion exists only to confirm interaction, guide attention, or smooth transitions. It never decorates.

01
No Parallax, No Scroll-Jacking

The viewer controls the pace. Scroll-triggered animations, parallax layers, and scroll-jacked sections break the monograph metaphor. The page scrolls naturally. Images are encountered, not performed.

02
Hover: Brightness Only

Interactive images dim to brightness(0.82) on hover. Buttons shift color. Links shift color. No scale transforms on images. Scale(1.015) is acceptable on non-image interactive elements only. Duration: 150ms.

03
Transitions: Fade, Never Slide

Page transitions use opacity fade at 280ms with var(--ease-out). No sliding, no morphing, no shared-element transitions. The cut between views should feel like turning a page in a monograph — direct, not cinematic.

04
Stagger: 60ms for Grids

When a grid of images enters the viewport, stagger each item by 60ms. Use opacity only (0 to 1), no translateY. The grid builds left-to-right, top-to-bottom. Maximum 6 items staggered — the rest appear immediately.

09 — Accessibility

Inclusive by Default

Accessibility is structural, not cosmetic. Every contrast ratio, keyboard path, and screen reader annotation is documented and tested. Restraint in color makes accessibility easier — the near-achromatic palette reduces color-dependent information.

Pairing Foreground Background Ratio WCAG AA

Focus Rings

2px solid accent color with 2px offset. Focus rings appear on keyboard navigation (:focus-visible), not on mouse click. The ring uses var(--color-accent-500) on light backgrounds and var(--color-accent-300) on dark.

outline: 2px solid var(--color-accent-500);
outline-offset: 2px;
Gallery Keyboard Navigation

Arrow keys advance through images in lightbox view. Escape closes the lightbox. Tab moves between controls (previous, next, close, caption). The lightbox traps focus — tab does not escape to the page behind.

role="dialog" aria-modal="true" aria-label="Image viewer"
Previous: aria-label="Previous image"
Next: aria-label="Next image"
Image Alt Text

Describe composition and subject, not aesthetic judgment. "A woman standing at a rain-covered window, profile view, natural light" — not "Beautiful portrait photograph." Include the title and year if available: alt="Untitled #7, 2024. A woman standing at a rain-covered window."

Reduced Motion

When prefers-reduced-motion is active: no stagger on grid reveals (all items appear simultaneously), no shimmer on skeletons (static gray), no hover brightness transition (immediate state change). Opacity fades are allowed at reduced duration (60ms).

@media (prefers-reduced-motion: reduce) {
  *, *::before, *::after {
    animation-duration: 0.01ms !important;
    transition-duration: 0.01ms !important;
  }
}
Color Independence

No information is conveyed by color alone. Status badges include text labels. Error states include icon + text, not just a red border. Links within body text use underline-on-hover in addition to accent color. The near-achromatic palette makes this straightforward — the system relies on typography, spacing, and icons rather than color to create hierarchy.
