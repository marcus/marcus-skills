## 1. Script Structure Frameworks

### 1.1 Problem-Agitate-Solution (PAS)

The most widely used framework for software demo videos. It mirrors how people naturally think about problems and maps cleanly to a 60-90 second explainer.

**Structure:**

| Phase | Duration | Word Count (~) | Purpose |
|-------|----------|---------------|---------|
| Problem | 15-20 sec | 30-40 words | Name the pain point the audience recognizes |
| Agitate | 15-20 sec | 30-40 words | Show the consequences of inaction |
| Solution | 25-30 sec | 50-60 words | Introduce the product as the resolution |
| CTA | 5-10 sec | 10-20 words | Tell the viewer what to do next |

**Formula:**

```
[PROBLEM] "Managing remote teams through endless email chains creates chaos
and missed deadlines."

[AGITATE] "Every lost message means lost revenue. Every missed deadline means
frustrated clients. And your team? They're drowning."

[SOLUTION] "Meet [Product]. One workspace where your team communicates,
collaborates, and ships — without the chaos."

[CTA] "Start your free trial today."
```

**Why it works:** Videos using the problem-solving structure show 60% higher engagement. The agitation phase creates emotional investment before the product appears.

### 1.2 Before-After-Bridge (BAB)

Shows the transformation your product enables. Particularly effective for demos with strong visual contrast.

**Structure:**

```
BEFORE: "Here's what your world looks like today." (Show the pain)
AFTER:  "Here's what it could look like." (Show the outcome)
BRIDGE: "Here's how [Product] gets you there." (Show the product)
```

**Best for:** Products with visually demonstrable outcomes (dashboards, design tools, analytics platforms).

### 1.3 Tell-Show-Tell

A three-phase presentation method that increases information retention by up to 65%.

| Phase | Duration | What Happens |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Tell (Set Context) | 30-60 sec | Outline what the audience will see and why it matters |
| Show (Demonstrate) | 3-5 min per segment | Live product demonstration with real-world scenarios |
| Tell (Recap) | 30-60 sec | Reinforce value, link features back to business impact |

**Implementation tips:**
- Use presenter notes for consistency
- For complex products, break into multiple Tell-Show-Tell modules rather than one long demo
- Focus summaries on business impact rather than technical specs
- Keep each "Show" segment under 5 minutes to avoid perceived complexity

### 1.4 Hero's Journey (Adapted for Product Demos)

Position your customer as the hero and your product as the guide (the "Yoda" or "Dumbledore" role).

**Three-Act Structure:**

```
ACT I — The Ordinary World (Setup)
  The hero (your customer) lives in a world with a specific challenge.
  "Sarah manages a 50-person sales team. Every quarter, forecasting
  takes two weeks of spreadsheet wrangling."

ACT II — The Journey (Confrontation + Discovery)
  The hero discovers a new tool (your product) and begins using it.
  This is where the demo lives. Show the product solving real problems.
  "Then Sarah found [Product]. In her first week, she..."

ACT III — The Return (Resolution)
  The hero's world is transformed. Show measurable outcomes.
  "Now Sarah's team forecasts in hours, not weeks. They closed
  23% more deals last quarter."
```

**Key principle:** Your company is NOT the hero. The customer is. Your product is the mentor that enables their transformation.

### 1.5 Job-to-be-Done (JTBD) Narrative

Centers the script on the outcome the customer is hiring the product to achieve, not the features.

**Formula:**

```
SITUATION:  "When [situation]..."
MOTIVATION: "I want to [job to be done]..."
OUTCOME:    "So that [desired outcome]..."
PRODUCT:    "[Product] does exactly this by [mechanism]."
```

**Example:**

```
"When your engineering team ships a new release, you want to know
instantly if anything breaks — so your customers never notice a problem.
[Product] monitors every deployment in real time and alerts you in
Slack before users are impacted."
```

### 1.6 Hook-Story-Offer

A concise three-part framework especially effective for short-form video.

```
HOOK:  Capture attention with a bold question or claim (2-5 sec)
STORY: Engage with a mini-narrative showing the journey (20-40 sec)
OFFER: Close with a clear call-to-action (5-10 sec)
```

**Example:**
```
HOOK:  "Are your teams wasting hours on manual fixes?"
STORY: "One of our clients saved 1,000+ hours in their first quarter
        by automating their deployment pipeline with [Product]."
OFFER: "Schedule your demo today and see what you could save."
```

### 1.7 AIDA Model

Guides audiences through the buyer journey stages.

| Stage | Purpose | Video Application |
|-------|---------|-------------------|
| Attention | Grab focus | Shocking stat or bold visual |
| Interest | Build relevance | Show the problem is real and widespread |
| Desire | Create want | Demonstrate the product solving the problem |
| Action | Drive conversion | Clear CTA with next step |

### 1.8 Pixar's Story Spine

A sequential narrative structure that creates emotional engagement:

```
"Once upon a time... [status quo]"
"Every day... [routine/problem]"
"One day... [discovery of product]"
"Because of that... [first improvement]"
"Because of that... [compounding benefit]"
"Until finally... [transformed outcome]"
```

### 1.9 Simon Sinek's Golden Circle

Start with WHY, then HOW, then WHAT.

```
WHY:  "We believe teams should spend time on work that matters,
       not manual data entry."
HOW:  "We built an AI that automates repetitive workflows so your
       team can focus on strategy."
WHAT: "Our platform saves teams an average of 200 hours per month."
```

### 1.10 Freytag's Pyramid (Five-Act Structure)

For longer, more dramatic narratives:

```
1. Exposition    — Introduce the character and their world
2. Rising Action — Show the problem escalating
3. Climax        — The critical turning point (product introduction)
4. Falling Action — Show the solution working
5. Resolution    — Measurable, transformed outcome
```

## 2. Opening Hooks

### 2.1 The Critical Window

- You have **5-10 seconds** to convince viewers to stay
- A strong hook retains **70% of viewers** past 10 seconds
- A weak hook loses **more than half** your audience immediately
- Over **33% of viewers** drop off in the first 30 seconds if the intro is not engaging
- Videos holding 70%+ retention in the first 30 seconds rank significantly higher on platforms

### 2.2 Seven Proven Hook Techniques

#### 1. Bold Problem Statement
State a known pain point directly.
```
"Tired of spending your marketing budget on ads that don't convert?"
```
- Best for: B2B SaaS with problem-aware audiences
- Creates immediate relevance; viewer thinks "They get me"

#### 2. Shocking Statistic
Lead with surprising data that disrupts expectations.
```
"90% of customer support tickets are questions already answered
in your docs. What if AI could handle them?"
```
- Best for: Educating audiences about problem scale

#### 3. Direct Question
Pose a specific, niche-targeted question that forces mental engagement.
```
"Are you a SaaS founder struggling to get investors interested
in your Series A?"
```
- Best for: Well-defined, specific audience segments

#### 4. Provocative Statement
Challenge conventional wisdom with contrarian positioning.
```
"Your beautiful website is actually costing you sales."
```
- Best for: Products offering fundamentally different approaches

#### 5. Relatable Story (Micro-Narrative)
Build an emotional connection in seconds.
```
"Meet Jane. Her explainer video was putting prospects to sleep.
Her conversion rate? 0.3%. Then she tried something different."
```
- Best for: Complex, emotionally resonant problems

#### 6. Visual Surprise / Pattern Interrupt
Deploy striking imagery that breaks the autopilot before audio even registers.
- Rapid zoom, sudden color change, or freeze-frame with text overlay
- Consistently outperforms static openings
- Videos using a pattern interrupt in the first 5 seconds show **23% higher retention**
- Best for: Visually crowded platforms; requires high-quality production

#### 7. Immediate Value Proposition
State the core benefit directly with no buildup.
```
"Create studio-quality explainer videos in under a week.
Here's how."
```
- Best for: Solution-aware, bottom-funnel audiences

### 2.3 Cold Open vs. Brand Intro

**Cold opens win.** The data is consistent:

| Approach | Retention Impact |
|----------|-----------------|
| Cold open (jump into content) | Highest retention; creates mystery and momentum |
| Question or statement hook first, then brand | Strong retention; brand context comes after engagement |
| Animated logo intro first | Significantly lower retention; viewers click away |
| Slow build with context/setup | Worst performer; "death for short-form content" |

**Best practice:** Hook first. Brand second. Cut traditional intros entirely or keep them under 5 seconds. Common culprits for early drop-off include slow intros, lengthy branding sequences, and burying the value too deep.

### 2.4 Opening Style Comparison

| Style | Strengths | Weaknesses | Best For |
|-------|-----------|------------|----------|
| Question-led | Forces mental engagement, creates relevance | Can feel formulaic if overused | Specific audience segments |
| Statement-led | Bold, memorable, sets authority | Needs to be genuinely surprising | Thought leadership, challenger brands |
| Visual-led | Works before audio registers, stops the scroll | Requires high production value | Social media, crowded feeds |
| Story-led | Emotional connection, memorable | Takes longer to deliver | Longer-form content, brand videos |
| Data-led | Credibility, surprises viewers | Can feel dry if not well-framed | Educational content, B2B audiences |

### 2.5 Hook Pacing

- Deliver your complete hook in **2 to 2.5 seconds** for short-form
- This leaves a half-second buffer before the critical 3-second mark
- Fast pacing signals to viewers the entire video will respect their time
- For longer videos, the hook can extend to 5-10 seconds but should still front-load value

## 3. Narration and Voiceover

### 3.1 Pacing: Words Per Minute

| Video Type | WPM Range | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|-------|
| Explainer video | 130-150 WPM | Sweet spot for comprehension + engagement |
| Corporate/narration | 120-150 WPM | Focus on clarity |
| Tutorial/technical | 120-130 WPM | Slower pace for complex information |
| Energetic/promo | 150-160 WPM | Faster for excitement, but risks comprehension |
| Maximum comfortable | 160 WPM | Beyond this, content becomes hard to follow |
| Minimum engaging | 120 WPM | Below this, pacing feels sluggish |

**Word count by video length:**

| Duration | Word Count |
|----------|-----------|
| 30 seconds | ~75 words |
| 60 seconds | 120-150 words |
| 90 seconds | 180-225 words |
| 2 minutes | 250-300 words |
| 3 minutes | 375-450 words |

**Key consideration:** Dense text with technical jargon or tongue twisters requires a slower pace. If your script contains complex terms, aim for 130 WPM to ensure comprehension.

### 3.2 Point of View

| POV | Effect | Best For | Data |
|-----|--------|----------|------|
| **Second person ("you")** | Draws viewers in, makes content conversational, helps viewers visualize themselves | Product demos, explainers, tutorials | University of Illinois study: second-person motivated more action; Journal of Interactive Marketing: enhances consumer involvement |
| **First person ("I/we")** | Creates intimacy and authenticity, feels personal | Customer stories, founder narratives, brand videos | CTAs in first-person voice had **90% better conversion rate** than second-person CTAs |
| **Third person ("they/the team")** | Creates objectivity, professional distance | Case studies, industry overviews, comparison videos | More formal, lower emotional engagement |

**Best practice for demos:** Use second person ("you") for the main narration to make viewers the protagonist. Switch to first person for CTAs ("Start my free trial" outperforms "Start your free trial").

### 3.3 Tone: Conversational vs. Professional

| Tone | Characteristics | Best For |
|------|----------------|----------|
| **Conversational** | Short sentences, contractions, direct address, natural phrasing | Explainers, product tours, startup brands |
| **Professional** | Polished, measured, authoritative, precise | Enterprise software, security products, regulated industries |
| **Enthusiastic** | Energetic, positive, forward-looking | Launch videos, feature announcements |
| **Empathetic** | Understanding, relatable, warm | Problem-focused intros, customer stories |

**Writing tip:** Write as you speak. Read your entire script aloud at natural pace before recording. Scripts that look good on paper often sound unnatural when spoken. If you stumble over a phrase, rewrite it.

### 3.4 On-Screen Presenter vs. Voiceover Only

| Approach | When to Use | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|----------|-------------|------------|---------------|
| **Voiceover only** | Software screencasts, tutorials, product tours | Viewer focus stays on the product; clean, professional feel; easier to update | Less personal connection; harder to convey emotion |
| **On-screen presenter** | Brand/culture videos, thought leadership, launch announcements | Builds trust and rapport; human connection; more engaging for talking-head content | Can distract from product UI; harder to update; higher production cost |
| **Hybrid** | Demo walkthroughs, feature highlights | Presenter introduces/concludes, product takes center stage during demo | Requires smooth transitions between formats |

**Research insight:** Studies show that while viewers express preference for seeing a presenter, there is no significant difference in learning performance between presenter-visible and voiceover-only formats. For software demos where the UI is the star, voiceover-only typically wins.

### 3.5 AI Voice vs. Human Voice

| Factor | AI Voice | Human Voice |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| **Cost** | 60-86% cheaper | Higher per-session cost |
| **Speed** | Instant generation, instant updates | Requires scheduling, recording sessions |
| **Emotional resonance** | Lower engagement in studies | Unmatched for emotional storytelling |
| **Authenticity** | Can feel flat or mismatched | Captures subtle tones, cultural nuance, humor |
| **Scalability** | Excellent for high-volume, multilingual | Limited by talent availability |
| **Trust** | Lower initial trust, improving rapidly | Higher brand trust, especially B2B |
| **Adoption trend** | 75% of branded Instagram content projected to use AI audio by 2026 | Remains standard for high-stakes content |

**Key findings:**
- Ads with AI voice elicit **lower consumer engagement** vs. human voices (TikTok data)
- Human voiceover **reduces cognitive load**, enhancing purchase intention
- AI-generated celebrity voices achieve engagement **comparable to human voices**
- Best practice: **Hybrid approach** — AI for scalable/straightforward content, human for emotional/high-stakes content

### 3.6 Voice Casting by Brand Personality

| Brand Personality | Voice Characteristics | Example Use |
|-------------------|----------------------|-------------|
| **Innovative/Tech-forward** | Clear, confident, medium pace, modern | Developer tools, AI products |
| **Warm/Approachable** | Friendly, conversational, smile-in-the-voice | Consumer SaaS, collaboration tools |
| **Authoritative/Enterprise** | Deep, measured, professional, baritone range | Security, finance, enterprise platforms |
| **Playful/Creative** | Energetic, expressive, varied inflection | Design tools, consumer apps |
| **Trustworthy/Reliable** | Calm, steady, clear enunciation | Healthcare tech, data platforms |
| **Bold/Disruptive** | Fast-paced, confident, provocative tone | Startup brands, challenger products |

**Casting tip:** A tech-savvy audience appreciates a clear, concise voice. A creative audience connects with passion and expressiveness. Always match the voice to your audience, not just your brand identity.

## 4. Content Strategy

### 4.1 The "One Big Idea" Principle

Every video should communicate **one core message**. Not three features. Not five benefits. One idea that the viewer remembers after the video ends.

**How to identify your one big idea:**
- What is the single most important thing a viewer should take away?
- If they forget everything else, what must they remember?
- Can you state it in one sentence?

**Application:**
```
WRONG: "Our platform has project management, time tracking,
        invoicing, team chat, and file storage."

RIGHT: "Stop switching between apps. Everything your team needs
        is in one place."
```

### 4.2 What Features to Show vs. Skip

| Show | Skip |
|------|------|
| Features that solve the audience's #1 pain point | Configuration screens and settings |
| Visually impressive workflows | Login/signup flows (unless that IS the feature) |
| "Aha moment" features that differentiate you | Every option in every menu |
| Features with clear before/after impact | Edge cases and advanced customization |
| Integrations with tools viewers already use | Backend/admin features (unless audience is admin) |

**Rule of thumb:** Highlight the features most valuable and frequently used. Do not overwhelm viewers with every capability. Prioritize what truly matters.

**For each feature shown, explain WHY it matters, not just WHAT it does.** Connect each feature to a tangible benefit.

### 4.3 Handling Complex Workflows

When your product involves multi-step processes:

1. **Break into segments** — Use multiple Tell-Show-Tell modules instead of one long walkthrough
2. **Use progressive disclosure** — Show the overview first, then drill into details
3. **Create visual anchors** — Use consistent UI highlights, callouts, or zoom effects to guide attention
4. **Narrate the "why" over the "how"** — Skip mechanical click-by-click narration; explain the purpose of each step
5. **Time-compress repetitive steps** — Speed up or skip routine actions with "And after completing the setup..."
6. **Limit to 3-5 key steps per video** — If the workflow has 12 steps, make multiple videos

### 4.4 Emotional vs. Rational Appeals

#### The 95% Rule (B2B Insight)

Neuroscience reveals that **95% of buying behavior is influenced by subconscious emotional processing**, not rational analysis. Buyers form gut reactions based on trust, fear of loss, aspiration, and relief, then seek data to support those choices.

| Emotion | How It Drives B2B Purchases | Video Technique |
|---------|---------------------------|-----------------|
| **Trust** | Reduces perceived risk when selecting a vendor | Customer testimonials, recognizable logos, data points |
| **Relief** | Promises fewer daily operational headaches | Before/after demos showing chaos vs. calm |
| **Pride** | Highlights career upside for the decision maker | "Be the one who brought this to your team" framing |
| **FOMO** | Accelerates action when peers adopt new tools | "Join 10,000+ teams already using..." |

#### B2B vs. B2C Approach

| | B2B | B2C |
|--|-----|-----|
| **Primary appeal** | Emotion first, justified with logic | Pure emotion, lifestyle transformation |
| **Decision process** | Committee-based, longer cycle | Individual, often impulsive |
| **Proof needed** | ROI data, case studies, integration compatibility | Reviews, social proof, visual appeal |
| **Script balance** | 60% emotional / 40% rational | 80% emotional / 20% rational |
| **Effective framing** | "Sleep better knowing your data is protected" > "Multi-layered encryption protocols" | "Look amazing in every photo" > "Advanced image processing algorithm" |

### 4.5 Social Proof Integration

**Types of social proof for software videos:**

| Type | Implementation | Placement |
|------|---------------|-----------|
| **Customer logos** | Grid of recognizable brand logos | After product introduction |
| **Metrics/numbers** | "Used by 50,000+ teams" or "4.8/5 on G2" | Opening hook or mid-video |
| **Video testimonials** | Short customer clips (10-15 sec each) | After feature demonstration |
| **Case study snippets** | "Company X reduced churn by 34%" | During or after solution section |
| **Integration logos** | Show logos of tools you integrate with | During solution section |

**Best practice for B2B:** Integrations are among the most important selling points for SaaS. If your product integrates with tools in the viewer's tech stack, showing those logos significantly increases conversion likelihood.

### 4.6 Creating Urgency Without Being Pushy

**Ethical urgency techniques:**

| Do | Do Not |
|----|--------|
| Highlight genuine limited-time offers with real deadlines | Use fake countdown timers that reset |
| Show what competitors/peers are already achieving | Create false scarcity ("Only 3 seats left!") |
| Frame around cost of inaction ("Every day you wait...") | Use high-pressure countdown language |
| Use social proof ("Teams are switching this quarter") | Stack multiple urgency tactics simultaneously |
| Be specific with deadlines and terms | Use vague timelines ("Act now!" with no specifics) |

**Implied urgency is more effective than explicit urgency.** Subtly hint at time sensitivity rather than demanding immediate action. For example:

```
PUSHY:  "Sign up NOW before this offer expires!"
BETTER: "Teams that started this quarter are already seeing 30%
         faster deploys. Where will you be next quarter?"
```

**Cooldown rule:** Once someone receives an urgency offer, they should not see another for at least 30 days to prevent urgency fatigue.

## 5. Video Types and When to Use Each

### 5.1 Video Type Matrix

| Video Type | Length | Funnel Stage | Primary Goal | Audience |
|-----------|--------|-------------|-------------|----------|
| **Explainer** | 60-90 sec | Top | "What is it and why should I care?" | Problem-aware prospects |
| **Product Tour** | 2-4 min | Middle | "How does it work end-to-end?" | Evaluating prospects |
| **Feature Highlight** | 30-90 sec | Middle | "What can this specific thing do?" | Existing users + evaluators |
| **Customer Story** | 60-120 sec | Bottom | "Who else uses it and what happened?" | Decision-stage buyers |
| **Launch/Announcement** | 30-90 sec | All | "What's new and why does it matter?" | Existing users + market |
| **Comparison** | 2-4 min | Bottom | "Why this over competitors?" | Active evaluators |
| **Quick Tip/Tutorial** | 30-90 sec | Post-sale | "How do I do this specific thing?" | Current users |
| **Culture/Brand** | 60-180 sec | Top | "Who are these people?" | Talent + brand-curious |

### 5.2 Explainer Video

**Purpose:** Introduce your product to people who have never heard of it or do not understand the category.

**Script structure (60-90 seconds):**
```
[0:00-0:05]  HOOK — Problem statement or surprising stat
[0:05-0:20]  PROBLEM — Describe the pain in relatable terms
[0:20-0:35]  AGITATE — Show consequences of the status quo
[0:35-0:55]  SOLUTION — Introduce product and show key capability
[0:55-1:10]  BENEFITS — 2-3 specific outcomes (with proof)
[1:10-1:20]  CTA — Clear next step
```

**Best practices:**
- SaaS explainer videos perform best at 60-90 seconds
- Focus on main benefits, skip lengthy feature rundowns
- Average viewer retention for 1-2 minute explainer clips is 57%
- Animated format works well for abstract concepts
- Live action builds authenticity for service-based offerings

### 5.3 Product Tour

**Purpose:** Show how the product works for people already interested enough to evaluate it.

**Script structure (2-4 minutes):**
```
[0:00-0:15]  HOOK — Restate the core value proposition
[0:15-0:45]  OVERVIEW — High-level view of the product
[0:45-2:30]  WALKTHROUGH — 3-5 key workflows demonstrated
              (Use Tell-Show-Tell for each)
[2:30-3:15]  RESULTS — What users achieve (metrics, testimonials)
[3:15-3:30]  CTA — Trial signup or demo booking
```

**Best practices:**
- Show real-world scenarios, not empty-state screens
- Populate with realistic data that viewers recognize
- Narrate the WHY over the HOW for each step
- Keep each workflow segment under 5 minutes

### 5.4 Feature Highlight

**Purpose:** Spotlight a specific capability for existing users or evaluators comparing options.

**Script structure (30-90 seconds):**
```
[0:00-0:05]  HOOK — Name the job this feature does
[0:05-0:15]  CONTEXT — When/why you'd use this feature
[0:15-0:50]  DEMO — Show the feature in action
[0:50-1:00]  BENEFIT — State the outcome clearly
[1:00-1:10]  CTA — "Try it now" or "Learn more"
```

**Best practices:**
- Go beyond function; explain WHY it is useful, not just WHAT it does
- Connect each feature to a tangible user benefit
- Start with the essentials; do not overwhelm

### 5.5 Customer Story / Testimonial

**Purpose:** Provide social proof from real users for people making a purchasing decision.

**Script structure (60-120 seconds):**
```
[0:00-0:10]  INTRO — Customer name, role, company
[0:10-0:25]  PROBLEM — What challenge were they facing?
[0:25-0:45]  JOURNEY — Why did they choose this product?
[0:45-1:15]  RESULTS — What measurable outcomes did they achieve?
[1:15-1:30]  RECOMMENDATION — Would they recommend it? Why?
```

**Best practice: Use guided questions, not rigid scripts.**

Effective questions to prompt authentic responses:
- "What was your biggest challenge before using [Product]?"
- "What made you choose [Product] over alternatives?"
- "How much time or money did you save?"
- "What surprised you most about the experience?"
- "What would you tell someone considering [Product]?"

**Key rules:**
- Ask open-ended questions, never yes/no
- Highlight measurable results (numbers make testimonials persuasive)
- Ideal length: 60-90 seconds
- Structure follows: Problem > Solution > Results

### 5.6 Launch / Announcement Video

**Purpose:** Generate excitement about something new for existing users and the broader market.

**Script structure (30-90 seconds):**
```
[0:00-0:05]  HOOK — Tease what's coming
[0:05-0:15]  CONTEXT — Why this matters / what problem it addresses
[0:15-0:45]  REVEAL — Show the new feature/product in action
[0:45-1:00]  IMPACT — What this means for users
[1:00-1:10]  CTA — Try it, learn more, join waitlist
```

**Best practices:**
- The first 15 seconds decide whether viewers keep watching
- Focus on benefits, not features
- User-centric narrative: challenge > resolution > outcome
- Include a specific call-to-action (pre-orders, webinar, demo)

### 5.7 Comparison Video

**Purpose:** Help active evaluators understand why your product is the right choice vs. alternatives.

**Script structure (2-4 minutes):**
```
[0:00-0:15]  HOOK — Frame the comparison ("Choosing between X and Y?")
[0:15-0:45]  CRITERIA — Define what matters (the evaluation framework)
[0:45-2:30]  COMPARISON — Side-by-side on each criterion
[2:30-3:00]  VERDICT — Honest summary of where each excels
[3:00-3:30]  CTA — Trial, demo, or detailed comparison page
```

**Critical rule:** Acknowledge competitor strengths. Viewers are skeptical of videos that unfairly disparage alternatives. Honest analysis builds credibility. Highlight where you provide a better or different solution, not where the competitor is bad.

### 5.8 Quick Tip / Tutorial

**Purpose:** Help current users accomplish a specific task. Drives retention and reduces support tickets.

**Script structure (30-90 seconds):**
```
[0:00-0:05]  HOOK — "Here's how to [accomplish task] in [Product]"
[0:05-0:10]  CONTEXT — When you'd want to do this
[0:10-0:50]  WALKTHROUGH — Step-by-step demonstration
[0:50-1:00]  RECAP — "Now you can [outcome]"
[1:00-1:10]  NEXT — Link to related tutorial or feature
```

### 5.9 Funnel Placement Summary

```
TOP OF FUNNEL (Awareness)
├── Explainer videos — Introduce the problem and your category
├── Brand/culture videos — Build familiarity and trust
└── Thought leadership — Establish authority

MIDDLE OF FUNNEL (Consideration)
├── Product tour — Show how it works end-to-end
├── Feature highlights — Deep dive on specific capabilities
└── Comparison videos — Why you vs. alternatives

BOTTOM OF FUNNEL (Decision)
├── Customer stories — Peer validation and social proof
├── Live demo recordings — Realistic usage scenarios
└── ROI/case study videos — Hard numbers and proof points

POST-SALE (Retention)
├── Quick tips/tutorials — Drive adoption and usage
├── Feature announcements — Re-engage and expand usage
└── Community/event content — Build loyalty
```

## 6. CTA and Conversion

### 6.1 CTA Placement by Position

| Position | Conversion | When to Use |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| **Pre-roll** (beginning) | Lowest; less than 1% of creators use exclusively | Short videos under 60 sec |
| **Mid-roll** (middle) | **Highest conversion rate** | Re-engage viewers who may drift; ideal for longer videos |
| **Post-roll** (end) | Strong for committed actions | Target viewers who watched the entire video |

**Key data:** Video CTAs across the Wistia platform have an average conversion rate of **~16%**. Video CTAs overall can increase conversion rates by **up to 80%** (Saleslion, 2019).

**Best practice:** Test multiple placements. The optimal position varies by video length, content type, and audience. A/B test pre-roll, mid-roll, and post-roll to find what works for your specific content.

### 6.2 CTA Language That Converts

**High-performing SaaS CTA phrases:**

| Category | Phrases |
|----------|---------|
| **Free trial** | "Start your free trial" / "Try it free" / "Get started free" |
| **Demo** | "Book a demo" / "See it in action" / "Watch the demo" |
| **Learn more** | "Find out more" / "See how it works" / "Explore features" |
| **Sign up** | "Get started" / "Join today" / "Get instant access" |
| **Urgency** | "Start your free 14-day trial today — no credit card required" |

**CTA optimization rules:**
1. Match the CTA to the video type (explainer = "Learn more"; demo = "Start free trial")
2. Both **say it in voiceover** AND show it as text/button on screen
3. Keep it short — a couple of words plus one clear action
4. Use first-person phrasing for buttons ("Start my free trial" > "Start your free trial")
5. Add friction-reducers ("No credit card required" / "Cancel anytime" / "Set up in 2 minutes")

### 6.3 Post-Video Experience Design

The viewer's journey does not end when the video stops. The post-video experience determines whether engagement converts to action.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Landing page messaging aligns with video messaging (no conflicting information)
- [ ] CTA on landing page matches CTA in video
- [ ] Video is placed above the fold for maximum visibility
- [ ] Page loads fast (video should not slow the page)
- [ ] Mobile-optimized video player
- [ ] Captions available (many users watch without sound)
- [ ] Related content or next video suggested
- [ ] Form is visible near the video (if lead generation is the goal)

### 6.4 Landing Page Video Placement

**Data:**
- Including video on a landing page can boost conversions by **over 86%**
- **30% of top-performing landing pages** incorporate video
- Marketers using video see **34% higher conversion rates**

**Placement best practices:**
- **Above the fold** — Position prominently before the scroll point
- **Near the CTA** — Proximity to the call-to-action maximizes conversion
- **Keep videos under 2 minutes** — Ideally 60-90 seconds
- **One key message** — Video should reinforce the page's primary message
- **Ensure alignment** — Video key points must match landing page copy
- **Autoplay consideration** — Generally avoid autoplay with sound; autoplay muted with captions can work
- **Mobile-first** — Ensure the video renders and plays correctly on mobile devices

## 7. Script Templates

### 7.1 The 60-Second Explainer Script Template

**Target: ~150 words / 130-150 WPM**

```
[HOOK — 5 seconds, ~15 words]
"What if you could [achieve desired outcome] without [pain point]?"

[PROBLEM — 15 seconds, ~35 words]
"Right now, [target audience] spend [time/money] on [painful activity].
It's [frustrating/slow/expensive], and it's holding your team back
from [what they actually want to do]."

[AGITATE — 10 seconds, ~25 words]
"And the longer you wait, the more [negative consequence].
Your competitors are already [moving ahead / solving this]."

[SOLUTION — 20 seconds, ~45 words]
"[Product] is [brief description in plain language].
Simply [key action 1], and [Product] automatically [key action 2].
In [timeframe], you'll [specific outcome]."

[SOCIAL PROOF — 5 seconds, ~15 words]
"[Number] teams already use [Product] to [key benefit].
[Optional: Named customer or rating]."

[CTA — 5 seconds, ~15 words]
"Start your free trial at [URL]. No credit card required."
```

### 7.2 The 90-Second SaaS Explainer Template

**Target: ~220 words / 130-150 WPM**

```
[HOOK — 5 seconds]
Bold question or surprising statistic.

[PROBLEM — 20 seconds]
Describe the status quo pain. Use "you" to make it personal.
Show what the viewer's day looks like with this problem.

[AGITATE — 15 seconds]
Escalate the consequences. What does this cost in time, money,
or frustration? What happens if nothing changes?

[SOLUTION INTRO — 10 seconds]
"That's why we built [Product]. [One-sentence description]."

[KEY FEATURES — 25 seconds]
Show 2-3 key features in action. For each:
  - Name the feature (2 sec)
  - Show it working (5 sec)
  - State the benefit (3 sec)

[SOCIAL PROOF — 10 seconds]
Customer quote, metric, or logo bar.
"[Company] reduced [problem metric] by [percentage] in [timeframe]."

[CTA — 5 seconds]
Clear, specific next step with friction-reducer.
"Try [Product] free for 14 days. Set up in 2 minutes."
```

### 7.3 The Product Tour Script Template

**Target: 2-3 minutes / ~300-400 words**

```
[COLD OPEN — 10 seconds]
Start with the outcome. Show the end result before explaining
how to get there. "This is what your dashboard looks like
after [Product] is set up. Let me show you how to get here."

[OVERVIEW — 30 seconds]
High-level product description. What is it? Who is it for?
Show the main interface or home screen.

[WORKFLOW 1 — 45 seconds]
TELL: "First, let's look at [workflow]. This is where you [task]."
SHOW: Demonstrate the workflow with realistic data.
TELL: "So now you've [outcome] in [timeframe] instead of [old way]."

[WORKFLOW 2 — 45 seconds]
Same Tell-Show-Tell pattern for second key workflow.

[WORKFLOW 3 — 45 seconds]
Same pattern for third key workflow.

[RESULTS — 15 seconds]
"Teams using [Product] typically see [metric 1], [metric 2],
and [metric 3]."

[CTA — 10 seconds]
"Ready to try it? Start your free trial at [URL],
or book a personalized demo with our team."
```

### 7.4 The Feature Highlight Script Template

**Target: 45-60 seconds / ~100-130 words**

```
[HOOK — 5 seconds]
"Here's how [Product] helps you [job to be done]."

[CONTEXT — 10 seconds]
"When you're [situation where this feature matters], you need
[capability]. That's exactly what [Feature Name] does."

[DEMO — 25 seconds]
Show the feature in action. Use realistic data.
Narrate the purpose of each step, not every click.
Keep it to 3-5 actions maximum.

[BENEFIT — 10 seconds]
"Instead of [old way], you now [new outcome].
That's [time saved / improvement metric]."

[CTA — 5 seconds]
"Try it now in your [Product] account, or start a free trial."
```

### 7.5 The Customer Story Script Template

**Target: 60-90 seconds / ~150-200 words**

```
[INTRO — 10 seconds]
"I'm [Name], [Role] at [Company]. We [brief company description]."

[CHALLENGE — 20 seconds]
"Before [Product], we were struggling with [specific problem].
We tried [alternative approaches], but [why they fell short].
It was costing us [specific impact: time/money/frustration]."

[DISCOVERY — 15 seconds]
"When we found [Product], what stood out was [key differentiator].
We could see right away that [initial impression]."

[RESULTS — 25 seconds]
"Since implementing [Product], we've [measurable result 1].
Our team [measurable result 2]. And we've [measurable result 3].
The biggest surprise was [unexpected benefit]."

[RECOMMENDATION — 10 seconds]
"If you're dealing with [problem], I'd absolutely recommend
[Product]. It [one-sentence summary of value]."
```

### 7.6 The Comparison Video Script Template

**Target: 2-3 minutes / ~300-400 words**

```
[HOOK — 10 seconds]
"Trying to decide between [Product] and [Competitor]?
Here's an honest breakdown to help you choose."

[FRAMING — 20 seconds]
"Both are [category] tools, but they take different approaches.
Let's compare them on [3-4 criteria that matter most]."

[CRITERION 1 — 30 seconds]
"For [criterion]: [Competitor] does [X]. [Product] does [Y].
If you need [specific need], [Product] is the stronger choice here."

[CRITERION 2 — 30 seconds]
Same structure. Be honest about where competitor excels.

[CRITERION 3 — 30 seconds]
Same structure.

[CRITERION 4 — 30 seconds]
Same structure.

[SUMMARY — 15 seconds]
"Choose [Competitor] if you need [specific thing].
Choose [Product] if you need [specific thing]."

[CTA — 15 seconds]
"Want to see [Product] in action? Book a demo or start a free trial.
Link in the description."
```

### 7.7 The Launch Announcement Script Template

**Target: 45-60 seconds / ~100-130 words**

```
[TEASE — 5 seconds]
"[Product] just got a major upgrade."

[CONTEXT — 10 seconds]
"You've been asking for [capability]. Today, we're delivering."

[REVEAL — 25 seconds]
Show the new feature/product in action.
Focus on 1-2 hero moments that demonstrate the value.
Use real-world data to make it tangible.

[IMPACT — 10 seconds]
"This means you can now [specific outcome] in [timeframe].
No more [old painful way]."

[CTA — 10 seconds]
"Available now for all [plan] users. Log in to try it,
or visit [URL] to learn more."
```
